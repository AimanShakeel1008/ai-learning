Core Supervised Algorithms

Chapter 5 complete (Lessons 14–18) — five model families, from lines to trees to memories to margins.

1What we learned, and what we added to the assistant

Chapter 4 gave us the rulebook: pipeline, honest splits, over/underfitting. Chapter 5 finally filled the "train" stage with real algorithms — five families of them, every one built from scratch by hand, so none of them is magic to you now.

  • Lesson 14 — linear regression. Predicting a number by fitting a line: prediction = weight × feature + bias. The line is found by gradient descent (Lesson 9's downhill walk) minimising mean squared error. First real learned model in the project: resolution_time.py, which estimates minutes-to-resolve from ticket length.
  • Lesson 15 — logistic regression. Predicting a category by squashing a weighted sum through the sigmoid into a probability between 0 and 1, trained with log loss, decided at a threshold that is a business choice, not math. It replaced the hand-written urgency rule in ticket_urgency.py with genuine learning.
  • Lesson 16 — decision trees and random forests. Predicting by asking learned yes/no questions, chosen greedily to minimise Gini impurity, with tree depth as the overfitting knob. A random forest tames single-tree jumpiness with bootstrap samples + random feature menus + voting. The tree became the project's category router, ticket_category.py.
  • Lesson 17 — gradient boosting. Trees built in sequence, each small stump fitted to the residuals — the to-do list of what all previous trees still get wrong — shrunk by a learning rate. It is gradient descent taken one tree at a time, and (as XGBoost/LightGBM) the usual champion on tabular data. Theory + notebook only.
  • Lesson 18 — kNN and SVM. Two more ways to decide: k-nearest neighbors stores everything and lets the k closest examples vote (k is the bias–variance dial; distance breaks without scaling), and the support vector machine picks the separating line with the widest margin, pinned only by its support vectors, with the kernel idea for shapes no straight line can split. Theory + notebook only.

Added to the assistant this chapter: resolution_time.py (Lesson 14) and an upgraded ticket_urgency.py (Lesson 15), then ticket_category.py (Lesson 16) — each with its companion notebook and self-check asserts, all wired into main.py. Lessons 17 and 18 deliberately left the project alone: their industrial versions arrive with scikit-learn in Chapter 9.

2How the chapter's ideas connect

The through-line: all five families answer the same question — "given features, what is the answer?" — but they disagree about what to learn. The weighted-sum family learns a few numbers and is fast, smooth, and explainable. The question-asking family learns thresholds, eats unscaled and mixed data happily, and (boosted) wins most tabular contests. The geometry-and-memory family learns almost nothing (kNN) or only the borderline (SVM) — and both live or die by distance, which is why feature scaling matters so much for them. Chapter 4's rules referee everything: whichever family you pick, its knobs get tuned on validation data, never on the test set.

3The story so far: how the assistant has grown

  • Phase 1 left the assistant with one toy: a word-counting urgency guesser. Everything else was tools and math — arrays, tables, plots, gradients, probability.
  • Chapter 4 added no code but installed the habits: pipeline, honest splits, reading the train-vs-validation gap.
  • Chapter 5 is where the assistant became genuinely machine-learned. It now has three trained capabilities: ticket_urgency.py (logistic regression → probability of urgency), resolution_time.py (linear regression → estimated minutes), and ticket_category.py (decision tree → shipping/billing/account routing). python main.py from inside customer-support-assistant/ still runs and self-checks all three end to end, exactly as it did after Lesson 16.
  • What we now know that the project doesn't yet use: forests, boosting, kNN, and SVMs — the alternatives we will line up against the current models with scikit-learn in Chapter 9, letting a validation set pick winners honestly.

5Ideas that come back later, and where

  • Logistic regression is secretly a single neuron. A weighted sum passed through a squashing function is exactly what one artificial neuron computes — Lesson 31 builds neural networks by stacking Lesson 15's core move, and Lessons 32–33 train them with the same gradient descent + loss recipe as Lessons 14–15.
  • Nearest-neighbor search returns as the engine of retrieval. Phase 6 (Lessons 63–64) finds the help articles closest to a customer's question in embedding space — kNN at industrial scale inside vector databases — and "similar customers/items" powers recommendations in Lesson 28.
  • Trees and boosting become the library workhorses. In Chapter 9 (Lessons 26–27) scikit-learn versions of this chapter's models get compared, tuned, and saved properly — and boosted trees stay the default strong answer for tabular business data.
  • The scaling trap gets its real treatment in Lesson 21. Lesson 14 met it in gradient descent and Lesson 18 showed it breaking distance outright; Lesson 21 covers scaling, imbalance, and leakage as their own discipline.
  • Every family's overfitting dial (depth, rounds, learning rate, k, C) becomes "hyperparameter tuning" — chosen with validation data and cross-validation in Lessons 23 and 27, without ever touching the test set.

6Self-check

  1. Linear and logistic regression both compute a weighted sum. What one step turns the first into the second, and what does the output mean after that step?
  2. A random forest and gradient boosting both use many trees. State the two ways they differ in how the trees are built and combined.
  3. Why does kNN with k = 1 score 100% on its own training data, and what does that number tell you about the model's real quality?
  4. Name the overfitting dial for each: a decision tree, gradient boosting, kNN, and a soft-margin SVM — and say which data (train / validation / test) you use to set each dial.
  5. What is a support vector, and what happens to an SVM's boundary if you delete a training point that is not one?

  1. Pass the weighted sum through the sigmoid, which squashes any number into the range 0–1. The output then reads as a probability of the positive class (e.g. P(urgent)), which you turn into a yes/no at a chosen threshold.
  2. How they are built: a forest builds its trees independently in parallel, each on a bootstrap sample with random feature menus; boosting builds trees in sequence, each one fitted to the residuals the previous trees left behind. How they combine: a forest votes/averages equal peers; boosting adds up ordered corrections, each shrunk by the learning rate.
  3. Because every stored point's nearest neighbor is itself (distance 0), so it always "predicts" its own label. It tells you nothing — only performance on held-out validation data measures real quality (Lesson 12's rule, again).
  4. Tree: depth (and minimum leaf size). Boosting: number of rounds and the learning rate (and stump/tree depth). kNN: k. SVM: C, the penalty for street violations (plus the kernel choice). All of them are set using the validation split — never the test set, which is looked at once at the very end.
  5. A support vector is a training point sitting exactly at the margin distance — on the street's edge — one of the few points that pin the boundary in place. Deleting a non-support-vector changes nothing: the widest street is defined only by the borderline points.

7Chapter challenge

Part 1 — pick the family. You are the ML engineer for the online store. For each task below, name the single best-fitting model family from this chapter and justify it in one or two sentences:

  1. Estimate how many minutes a ticket will take to resolve, from three numeric features whose relationship to time looks roughly straight-line.
  2. Predict customer churn from 50,000 rows of mixed tabular features, and squeeze out the best accuracy you can for a quarterly business report.
  3. The support team asks: "for each new ticket, just show us the three most similar past tickets and how they were resolved."
  4. Flag urgent tickets with a probability, so operations can move the alert threshold up or down as staffing changes.
  5. Route tickets into three categories with a model the support team can print as a wall-poster flowchart and check by eye.

Part 2 — work the numbers by hand. Four past tickets have a single "urgency score": not urgent at 2 and 3; urgent at 7 and 9. A new ticket arrives with score 4.

  1. What does kNN predict for the new ticket with k = 1? With k = 3?
  2. An SVM-style classifier considers the thresholds 4.5, 5.0, and 6.5 ("above → urgent"). Compute each margin, pick the winner, and name the support vectors.
  3. Does the winning threshold agree with the k = 3 vote on the new ticket? Say in one sentence how the two methods reached their (same or different) answers by completely different reasoning.
Click to reveal the answer

Part 1.

  1. Linear regression. A number to predict + a roughly straight-line relationship is exactly its home turf, and its weights tell you how many minutes each feature adds — cheap and explainable.
  2. Gradient boosting (XGBoost/LightGBM in practice). Large tabular data, accuracy is the priority: this is the family that usually wins there, with rounds/learning rate tuned on validation data.
  3. k-nearest neighbors with k = 3. The team literally asked for the three nearest neighbors — kNN's output is the product here (similar past cases plus their outcomes), not just a label.
  4. Logistic regression. It natively outputs a probability, and Lesson 15 taught that the alert threshold on that probability is a business dial — exactly what operations wants to move.
  5. A single decision tree, kept shallow. It is the one model that is a readable flowchart; a forest or boosted ensemble would be stronger but unprintable.

Part 2, step 1 — kNN. Distances from score 4: to 3 → 1, to 2 → 2, to 7 → 3, to 9 → 5. With k = 1 the nearest is 3 (not urgent) → NOT URGENT. With k = 3 the voters are 3 (not), 2 (not), 7 (urgent) → 2 votes to 1 → NOT URGENT.

Part 2, step 2 — the margins. A threshold's margin is its distance to the closest ticket; the closest not-urgent is 3 and the closest urgent is 7:

  • Threshold 4.5: gaps |3 − 4.5| = 1.5 and |7 − 4.5| = 2.5 → margin 1.5.
  • Threshold 5.0: gaps 2.0 and 2.0 → margin 2.0 — the widest, sitting exactly halfway between 3 and 7.
  • Threshold 6.5: gaps 3.5 and 0.5 → margin 0.5.

The winner is 5.0. The support vectors are the tickets at 3 and 7 — the borderline pair. (The tickets at 2 and 9 could move considerably without changing anything.)

Part 2, step 3. Yes — threshold 5.0 puts score 4 below the line: NOT URGENT, agreeing with the k = 3 vote. But the reasoning differs completely: kNN answered by polling the stored examples nearest to the new ticket, while the SVM answered by drawing the widest street between the two classes in advance and checking which side the ticket falls on — memory versus geometry, landing on the same verdict.
