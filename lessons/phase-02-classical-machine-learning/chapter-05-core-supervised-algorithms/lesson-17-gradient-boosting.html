Gradient Boosting

Many weak models correcting each other — the method that dominates tabular data.

Files created: lesson-notebooks/phase-02-classical-machine-learning/chapter-05-core-supervised-algorithms/lesson-17-gradient-boosting.ipynb

Prerequisites: Lesson 9 (gradient descent), Lesson 13 (overfitting), Lesson 14 (residuals, squared error, learning rate), Lesson 16 (decision trees, greedy splits, random forests)

1Why this matters

Last lesson ended with a promise: the random forest builds many trees independently and lets them vote — and there is a second, stronger way to build a team of trees. This lesson keeps that promise. Gradient boosting builds its trees in sequence, and each new tree has exactly one job: fix the mistakes that all the previous trees, combined, are still making.

That one change in team organization produces the family of models — XGBoost, LightGBM, and friends — that has dominated machine-learning competitions on tabular data (data that fits in a table: rows of tickets, orders, customers) for over a decade. When a data scientist at a real company faces a table of business data and wants the strongest classical model available, “try gradient boosting” is close to a reflex. If you only ever deeply understand two classical model families, make them linear models (Lessons 14–15) and boosted trees (this lesson).

For our support assistant, this is also a preview of how its predictions — resolution time, urgency, category — would be built if we used industrial-strength tools. And there is a beautiful bonus: the word “gradient” in the name is not a coincidence. This lesson closes a loop that started in Lesson 9 — boosting is gradient descent, taken one small tree at a time.

2Real-world analogy

Editing an essay in passes.

You write a first draft of an essay in five minutes. It is rough — but it exists. Then an editor reads it and marks, in red pen, exactly what is still wrong: “this paragraph too vague,” “this claim unsupported.” You make a revision pass that fixes only what the red pen marked. Then the editor reads the new version and marks what is still wrong — a shorter list now. Another pass fixes those. After several passes, the essay is sharp, even though no single pass was brilliant.

Mapping every piece:

  • The rough first draft = the starting model: one boring average, predicted for everyone.
  • The red-pen marks = the residuals: a precise list of what the current model still gets wrong, ticket by ticket.
  • One revision pass = one weak tree, trained on the red-pen marks — not on the original assignment.
  • Each pass reads the latest version = each tree is fitted to the errors left over after all previous trees — the work is sequential; pass 3 cannot be done before pass 2.
  • A careful editor changes only 30% of what they could = the learning rate: apply each correction only partially, so no single pass over-commits to its own opinion.
  • Knowing when to stop editing = the number of rounds: keep revising a finished essay and you start mangling good sentences — that is overfitting.

Contrast with Lesson 16's forest: the forest was five people writing five separate essays and voting on the best sentences. Boosting is one essay, polished by many passes.

3The concept explained

3.1 The starting point: the most boring model alive

Boosting begins with a “model” that ignores its input completely: always predict the average of the training answers. If our 8 past tickets took on average 24.5 minutes, predict 24.5 minutes — for every ticket, sight unseen.

Why the average and not some other number? Because we score models by squared error (Lessons 9 and 14), and among all single always-predicted numbers, the average is the one with the smallest total squared error. It is the best you can do without looking. Everything that comes after is about looking.

3.2 Residuals: the list of what is still wrong

The residual (met in Lesson 14) is actual answer minus predicted answer, one per training example. If a ticket actually took 8 minutes and we predicted 24.5, the residual is 8 − 24.5 = −16.5: we guessed 16.5 minutes too high. Positive residual = guessed too low; negative = too high.

The residuals are not just a score — they are a complete, per-ticket to-do list: “on this ticket, move your prediction down by 16.5; on that one, up by 21.5.” Boosting's central move is to treat that to-do list as a new training target.

3.3 The weak learner: a decision stump

The corrections are made by deliberately tiny trees. A decision stump is a decision tree allowed exactly one question — one split, two leaves. Ours asks “is the ticket length ≤ some threshold?”, and each side answers with a single number.

Why choose a weak piece on purpose? Because the plan is many small corrections. Each stump fixes a bit of what is wrong; whatever it misses stays in the residuals for the next stump. A strong model that could fix everything in one gulp would also swallow the noise in one gulp (Lesson 13's overfitting). Weak pieces added gradually are easy to keep under control. Boosting literature literally calls them weak learners.

Fitting a stump is Lesson 16's greedy split search, refueled for regression:

  1. Candidate thresholds = midpoints between neighbouring lengths (15, 25, …, 75).
  2. Each threshold splits tickets into a left pile (≤) and right pile (>).
  3. Each side's answer = the average of its pile (best single number, same reason as 3.1).
  4. Score = squared error left over. Lowest wins.

Word question → number question; majority label + Gini → average + squared error. Same machine.

3.4 The boosting loop, worked with real numbers

Our notebook data (lengths in words → minutes to resolve — note the jumps, this is not a straight line):

length:  10  20  30  40  50  60  70  80
minutes:  8  10  14  16  28  30  44  46

Round 0. Average = 196 ÷ 8 = 24.5. Residuals: −16.5, −14.5, −10.5, −8.5, +3.5, +5.5, +19.5, +21.5. Total squared error: 1550.0.

Round 1. Fit a stump to the residuals. Trying every threshold, “length ≤ 45?” wins by a mile: its left pile's residuals average −12.5 and its right pile's average +12.5 (the data's biggest jump is between 40 and 50 words). Add the correction:

new prediction = old prediction + stump's correction
left of 45:  24.5 + (−12.5) = 12.0 minutes
right of 45: 24.5 + (+12.5) = 37.0 minutes

Total squared error: 1550 → 300. One yes/no question removed 81% of the error.

Round 2. Recompute residuals against the NEW predictions: −4, −2, +2, +4, −9, −7, +7, +9. The biggest remaining structure is on the right side (70–80-word tickets are still 7–9 minutes underestimated). Best stump: “length ≤ 65?” → left −2.67, right +8.0. Error: 300 → 129.3.

Round 3. Best stump: “length ≤ 45?” again, now with small values (+2.67 / −2.67) — it is fine-tuning a region it already corrected once. Error: 129.3 → 72.4.

Each round: (1) compute residuals, (2) fit a stump to them, (3) add its corrections to the model, (4) repeat. The final model is a recipe:

prediction(ticket) = 24.5                      <- the average
                   + stump1(ticket)            <- ±12.5 at the 45-word line
                   + stump2(ticket)            <- fixes the right side
                   + stump3(ticket)            <- fine-tuning
                   + ...

Notice what makes this sequential: stump 2's training target (the round-2 residuals) did not exist until stump 1 was built and applied. No tree can be trained before its predecessors. Forest trees are jurors voting in parallel; boosting trees are a relay team.

3.5 The learning rate: take smaller steps on purpose

Real boosting rarely applies a stump's full correction. It multiplies every correction by a small number — the learning rate, also called shrinkage (typical values 0.3, 0.1, or smaller):

new prediction = old prediction + learning_rate × correction

Why deliberately weaken your own corrections? Each correction was measured on the training tickets alone — partly real pattern, partly noise (Lesson 13). A small step means no single stump stamps its full opinion (noise included) onto the model. The real pattern is not lost: whatever a small step leaves unfixed is still sitting in the residuals, so later rounds measure it again and keep correcting it. Real structure gets repeatedly confirmed; one-off noise does not. The price: more rounds needed — which is why production boosted models use hundreds of small trees.

This is the same care-versus-speed dial as gradient descent's learning rate in Lessons 9 and 14 — and that is no coincidence:

3.6 Why “gradient” boosting

Lesson 9: the gradient is the steepest-uphill direction on the error surface, and gradient descent means “step a little downhill, repeat.” Now look at what boosting does. For squared error, the direction each prediction should move to reduce its error fastest is exactly its residual — “you predicted 12, the answer is 16” means “move up by 4.” Fitting a stump to the residuals and adding a small multiple of it is therefore a gradient-descent step — except the step is taken by adding a whole small tree, instead of nudging a weight and a bias. Boosting is gradient descent where each “step” is a tree. (For losses other than squared error, libraries fit each tree to a generalized residual; the idea is identical.)

3.7 Where boosting overfits — and its knobs

A forest's extra trees are just extra independent opinions in an average — more trees is roughly always safer. Boosting's extra trees keep actively hunting whatever error remains — and once the real pattern is captured, the only thing left to hunt is noise. Boosting can drive training error to zero and keep “improving” while quietly getting worse on new data. Its overfitting knobs:

  • number of rounds (more trees = more capacity),
  • learning rate (smaller = more careful),
  • depth of each tree (deeper = stronger pieces = more capacity).

All tuned with a validation set (Lesson 12); the standard trick is early stopping — watch validation error each round, stop adding trees when it stops improving.

3.8 The real libraries

Nobody hand-rolls this in production. XGBoost and LightGBM are the famous stand-alone implementations; scikit-learn ships a histogram gradient-boosting model too. They run exactly the loop above, plus: trees a few levels deep instead of stumps, hundreds of rounds at small learning rates, random subsets of rows/columns per tree (the forest's tricks, borrowed), regularization penalties on the trees, early stopping, and serious speed engineering.

Library names and argument details change over time — treat any specific API in this lesson as something to verify against the official docs.

6Applied to our assistant

No project change this lesson — on purpose. The assistant's resolution-time data (Lesson 14) was built to sit on a straight line, so its linear model already fits it well; bolting a from-scratch stump booster onto the project would be replacing a good model with a teaching prop.

Gradient boosting earns its keep on bigger, messier tables of features — and doing it justice means using a real library (scikit-learn/XGBoost-class tools), which arrive later in the course. When the assistant's models graduate to real libraries, boosted trees will be a serious candidate for its tabular predictions; today's lesson is the understanding that will make that upgrade a five-line change instead of magic.

The project still runs exactly as before: python main.py from inside customer-support-assistant/ exercises urgency, resolution time, and category routing, unchanged.

7Common mistakes and gotchas

  • Fitting each new tree to the answers instead of the residuals. Then every tree learns the same thing and adding them makes predictions absurdly large. The target of round n is what is still wrong after rounds 1 to n−1 — that subtlety is the entire algorithm.
  • Treating “more trees” as safe, like a forest. In a forest, extra trees just stabilize the average. In boosting, extra rounds add capacity and will eventually memorize noise. Boosting needs early stopping or a tuned round count; a forest mostly doesn't.
  • Cranking the learning rate up to train faster. Big steps let individual trees stamp their noise into the model. The reliable recipe is the opposite: small learning rate + many rounds + early stopping.
  • Using strong (deep) trees as the weak learner. Depth-8 trees each gulp big chunks of pattern-plus-noise, and boosting compounds them fast. Real setups keep trees shallow (commonly ~3–8 levels; our notebook uses depth 1) so control stays with the loop, not the pieces.
  • Expecting smooth predictions. A sum of trees is a staircase: inputs between two thresholds all get identical predictions, and beyond the outermost threshold the prediction goes flat — same extrapolation blindness as Lesson 16's trees.

8When to use this, and tradeoffs

Reach for gradient boosting when the data is a table of mixed features and you want the strongest classical accuracy available — it is the default “serious model” for tabular business problems (churn, fraud, pricing, our ticket predictions at scale), usually edging out random forests once tuned. Everything trees gave you carries over: no feature scaling needed, mixed feature types welcome.

The costs: it must be tuned (rounds, learning rate, depth — a forest is much more forgiving out of the box); it trains sequentially, so it is harder to speed up by throwing machines at it; a 300-tree sum is not human-readable the way Lesson 16's single printed tree was; and because it chases residuals, it will chase mislabeled examples too — noisy labels hurt boosting more than they hurt a forest. On tiny datasets (like our 8 tickets!) it happily overfits; and for text-heavy meaning problems, later phases' neural models take over. Honest default: try a random forest first as a robust baseline, then boosting when you want to squeeze out more accuracy and are willing to tune.

9Key takeaways

  • Gradient boosting starts from one boring guess (the average) and repeatedly fits a small weak tree to the current residuals, adding a scaled copy of its corrections to the model, so the final model is the average plus a long sum of small corrections.
  • Each tree is trained on the errors left over after all previous trees, which makes boosting strictly sequential — the opposite of a random forest's independent trees voting in parallel.
  • For squared error, the residual is exactly the steepest-downhill direction for each prediction, so boosting is gradient descent in which every step is a whole small tree — that is what “gradient” in the name means.
  • The learning rate shrinks every correction so that no single tree can stamp its noise onto the model, at the price of needing many more rounds; rounds, learning rate, and tree depth are boosting's overfitting knobs, tuned with a validation set and early stopping.
  • In practice you use a library — XGBoost, LightGBM, or scikit-learn's histogram gradient boosting (verify current APIs against official docs) — and this family is usually the strongest classical choice on tabular data.

Round 2 would likely pick threshold 25 again: half of the jump is still unfixed, so the biggest remaining structure is still “short side too high, long side too low.” That is the learning-rate trade in miniature — each pass fixes less, but the still-unfixed part stays on the to-do list (the residuals) and gets corrected again on later passes.
